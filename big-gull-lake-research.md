# Big Gull Lake / Clarendon Lake: Comprehensive Research Brief

Big Gull Lake � formerly known as **Clarendon Lake** [^1][^3] � sits in Frontenac County, eastern Ontario, straddling the townships of North Frontenac and Central Frontenac, near the villages of Arden and Harlowe [^1]. It is part of one of the most geologically ancient, ecologically unique, and historically layered landscapes in all of Canada.

---

## I. Physical Geography & Dimensions

### Lake Dimensions

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Length | ~21�24 km (13�15.6 miles) [^4][^5] |
| Width | ~2.5 km (1.6 miles) [^5] |
| Surface Area | ~5,979 acres (2,364 hectares) [^4][^5] |
| Shoreline Perimeter | 88�104 km (55�65 miles) [^4][^5] |
| Maximum Depth | 26 m (85 ft) [^5] |
| Average Depth | ~3.9 m (12.9 ft) [^4] |
| Elevation (lake surface) | ~252�256 m above sea level [^6][^7] |
| Elevation Range (surrounding terrain) | 216�299 m [^6] |

### Hydrology

Big Gull Lake is a **headwater lake** � it sits near the top of the watershed. It drains through a dam at Gull Creek into Crotch Lake to the northeast [^1][^2]. The lake consists of two distinct basins: the deeper, rockier **eastern basin** and the shallower **western section** (sometimes called Little Gull Lake). The eastern basin favours walleye and smallmouth bass, while the western basin favours largemouth bass and pike [^4].

## II. Geology � 2.5 Billion Years in the Making

Big Gull Lake sits directly on the **Precambrian Canadian Shield**, carved from some of the oldest exposed rock on Earth. The North Frontenac Parklands, which includes Big Gull Lake, are described as "carved from the Precambrian Granite of the Canadian Shield" and "were 2� billion years in the making" [^8][^9].

### Geological Age & Formation

- The bedrock belongs to the **Grenville Province**, a massive belt of metamorphic and igneous rock stretching from Labrador through Quebec, Ontario, and into northern New York State [^10][^11].
- These rocks are the **eroded roots of an ancient mountain range** (the Laurentian Mountain Belt) formed approximately **1.0�1.3 billion years ago** during the assembly of the supercontinent **Rodinia**, when a continental mass collided with the ancient core of North America (Laurentia) [^11].
- The collision pushed older Precambrian rocks (1.8�2.5 billion years old) deeper into the crust, where they were deformed by immense heat and pressure, then thrust upward [^11].

### Rock Types

The bedrock is a complex of high-grade metamorphic rocks [^10][^11][^12]:

- **Granite and granitic gneiss** � the dominant pink-grey rock visible along shorelines and on island outcrops
- **Quartz-biotite-feldspar gneiss**
- **Migmatites** � layered gneisses invaded by narrow granitic bands parallel to the layering
- **Crystalline limestone (marble)**
- **Quartzite**
- **Schist**

The region is technically part of the **Central Metasedimentary Belt (CMB)** of the Grenville Supergroup � a belt of metamorphosed sediments representing ancient crustal fragments ("terranes" or "domains") thrust together over time [^11].

### The Frontenac Arch

Big Gull Lake sits within the broader **Frontenac Arch** � an hourglass-shaped outlier of the Precambrian Shield that extends southward through Ontario and into the U.S., connecting the Canadian Shield and boreal forest to the Adirondack and Appalachian Mountains [^11][^12]. Where this arch meets the St. Lawrence River, it creates the Thousand Islands [^11]. The Frontenac Arch is a **UNESCO-designated Biosphere Reserve**, recognized as arguably Canada's most biodiverse region [^13][^14].

A notable feature nearby is a **meteor crater near Holleford** where the shield meets limestone plains [^12].

### Glaciation � The Last 20,000 Years

- Around 20,000 years ago, the **Laurentide Ice Sheet** (several kilometres thick) covered the entire region. The eastern Labrador lobe flowed southwesterly, roughly parallel to the geological grain of the Grenville rocks [^11].
- Between ~17,000�14,000 years ago, the ice began retreating northward [^11].
- By ~12,500 years ago, **glacial Lake Iroquois** (the ancestor of Lake Ontario) had formed to the west [^11].
- The **Champlain Sea** � an arm of the Atlantic Ocean � flooded into the region as the ice retreated, depositing marine clays and silts before disappearing by ~9,000 years ago [^11].
- By ~6,000 years ago, the modern Great Lakes and Ottawa/St. Lawrence drainage patterns were established [^11].
- Glaciers scoured the basins, polished exposed rock surfaces, deposited glacial tills (sandy tills from Precambrian rock erosion), and created the characteristic landscape of rolling hills, rugged cliffs, exposed granite knobs, and countless lakes that define the region today [^11][^15].

### Soils

The soils of the Frontenac Arch are reflective of the underlying Precambrian rock � generally **thin, rocky, and infertile** over the Shield, in contrast to the fertile lands to the east and south that were formerly covered by the inland Champlain Sea [^11][^16].

## III. Flora � Trees & Vegetation

Big Gull Lake sits at one of the most ecologically significant crossroads on the continent. The Frontenac Arch creates a junction where **five forest regions of eastern North America** meet and intermingle [^11][^13]:

1. Great Lakes � St. Lawrence forest
2. Boreal forest
3. Appalachian forest
4. Atlantic Coastal forest
5. Carolinian forest

### Dominant Tree Species

**Conifers:** White pine, red pine, jack pine, balsam fir, white spruce, black spruce, eastern white cedar, hemlock, and notably **pitch pine** � a rare Atlantic coastal species that reaches an extreme western range limit here [^11][^12].

**Hardwoods:** Sugar maple, red oak, white birch, yellow birch, trembling aspen, red maple, beech, and ironwood. The gypsy moth caterpillar invasion of 1985 devastated the oaks around Big Gull Lake specifically [^17].

**Rare southerly species (Carolinian):** Pignut hickory, buttonbush, summer grape [^11].

**Atlantic species:** Wire birch, red spruce [^11].

**Boreal species:** Sweet gale, barren-ground strawberry, balsam fir [^11].

The shallow soils and recurring fire create unusual **fire barren communities** supporting rare plants like bear oak and deerberry [^12].

### Forest History

Nine thousand years ago, the forests were primarily coniferous. Hardwoods such as hemlock didn't appear until about **7,500 years ago** as the climate warmed [^18]. The rugged terrain creates millions of micro-habitats � sunny slopes, sheltered valleys, windswept ridges, boggy wetlands � each supporting different plant communities [^11]. Lake Ontario acts as a great moderator of climate, prolonging the growing season and allowing species normally found further south to survive here [^11].

The 1840s�1880s saw massive **timber cutting** that transformed the landscape. Mature pine forests were cut for shipbuilding timber until steel plates replaced wooden timbers in the 1880s [^19]. Unsuitable wood was piled and burned to produce **potash** � a major trade commodity � with 519 asheries across Canada by 1850 burning an estimated 2.81 billion pounds of hardwood annually [^16].

## IV. Fauna & Wildlife

### Fish Species (Big Gull Lake)

- Walleye (stocked from 1925�1951, with up to 2 million fish planted in 1946) [^3]
- Northern pike, smallmouth bass, largemouth bass, perch [^1][^2]
- Lake whitefish, lake herring [^1]
- Historical stockings also included lake trout and muskellunge (1916�1961) [^3]
- Walleye spawning requires particular management due to limited rocky shoal areas. The dam at Gull Creek maintains water levels to cover spawning areas during breeding season [^1].

### Birds

- Big Gull Lake is renowned as a **nesting area for hawks and ospreys** [^2]
- Common loons (populations monitored via volunteer surveys) [^20]
- Eagles (vulnerable to lead from fishing tackle) [^21]
- The broader Frontenac Arch hosts the nationally endangered **cerulean warbler** and has an **Important Bird Area** designation from Bird Studies Canada [^11][^12]

### Mammals

- Moose, black bear, white-tailed deer [^22]
- Both **southern and northern flying squirrels** coexist (southern in deciduous forest, northern in mixed coniferous) [^11]
- Both **cottontail rabbits** (a southerly species) and **snowshoe hares** coexist [^11]
- Red fox, raccoon, red squirrel, coyote [^22]
- Occasional opossums crossing northward through the Arch [^11]

### Reptiles

- The **eastern ratsnake** (Central ratsnake) � one of its last remaining population areas globally. The Frontenac Arch's climate, rocky dens for winter refuge, and food sources continue to provide suitable conditions [^11].
- **Five-lined skink** � at the northern limit of its range [^12]

### Endangered Species

North Frontenac hosts numerous at-risk species including mountain lion, golden eagle, lake sturgeon, and several bat species (tricoloured bat, northern myotis, eastern small-footed myotis) [^23]. The Frontenac Arch region supports **34 species at risk** (plants and animals combined) [^11].

## V. Islands & Natural Features

Big Gull Lake features **many islands** throughout its length. Twenty-five island campsites are accessible only by watercraft through the North Frontenac Parklands system [^2]. The islands feature:

- Exposed Precambrian granite and gneiss shorelines
- Windswept pines clinging to rock outcrops
- Hidden coves with driftwood art [^24]
- Rocky shoals critical for walleye spawning � cottagers and MNR have placed **hundreds of tons of rubble** on shoals since the late 1980s to rebuild spawning habitat [^17]

The North Frontenac Parklands � which encompasses Big Gull Lake � resides entirely on **Crown Land** and is described as "one of the most pristine natural environments in the Province" [^9].

Much of the shoreline is private (cottages and resorts) except for Crown land along the north-central shore [^4]. Access is available at both ends: the east end via Helen Lane off Ardoch Road, and the west end via Thompson Road off Big Gull Lake Road near Harlowe [^1][^2].

## VI. Indigenous History � Thousands of Years of Stewardship

### Territory

The land around Big Gull Lake is situated on **ancestral Haudenosaunee lands** and **unceded Algonquin Anishinaabe territory** [^18]. The Algonquin people (self-identified as **Om�mawi'inini**) have inhabited the Ottawa River watershed spanning eastern Ontario and western Quebec for at least **10,000�12,000 years** [^25][^26].

A **1720 French map** ("Carte de la Nouvelle France" by Gerard van Keulen) explicitly identifies eastern and central Upper Canada as Algonquin territory [^16]. **No known treaties were ever made between First Nations and European settlers in the Frontenac Arch region itself** � the land remains unceded [^18].

### Way of Life

- The Algonquin people prospered through **hunting, trapping, and gathering wild foods** for thousands of years, developing intricate governance systems based on family units represented by a Grand Council [^16].
- The mosaic of lakes and network of rivers provided the framework for **settlement, transportation, and resource management** [^18].
- **Birch bark canoes** were the primary summer transportation � constructed by sewing birch bark strips to wooden frames using spruce root laces, sealed with spruce gum. They were lightweight, ideal for portaging between lakes, and easily repaired [^27].
- In winter, travel shifted to **snowshoes and toboggans** [^27].
- The Ottawa River � known to Algonquins as **Kitchisippi** ("Big River") � served as their "main artery," with interconnected tributaries providing endless travel routes for hunting, fishing, gathering plants, camping, and trading [^26].
- The St. Lawrence watershed served as the basis of **continental-scale trade routes** for early Indigenous peoples [^18].

### Archaeological Evidence

- Settlement in the broader region has been **practically continuous** for thousands of years [^18].
- The **Wyght site** on Lower Rideau Lake contains an Early Archaic component dated to **6000 BC** [^28].
- **Frontenac Island** contains significant burial sites with visits dating to the **Early Archaic period (8,000�10,000 years ago)**, including a woman's grave accompanied by a wood turtle shell (~4000 BC), a unique group burial of two adults and two infants below limestone slabs with Laurentian projectile points, and a bone comb [^29].

### The Dish with One Spoon (1701)

The **Dish with One Spoon wampum agreement** (Gdoo-naaganinaa) was made between Haudenosaunee and Anishinaabe nations, extending from Montreal to Fort Erie. The concept of a shared dish with one spoon described how land could be shared between nations for the mutual benefit of all inhabitants. This contributed to the creation of the "Great League of Peace" � the Haudenosaunee Confederacy made up of the Seneca, Cayuga, Oneida, Onondaga, Mohawk, and later Tuscarora nations [^18].

### Colonial Dispossession

- The **Royal Proclamation of 1763** recognized pre-existing Indigenous land rights and stipulated that only the Crown could negotiate land transactions with Indigenous peoples [^16][^18].
- Despite this, land was systematically appropriated. Algonquin leaders petitioned the colonial government starting in **1772** and continuing for over two centuries [^30].
- In **1798**, Algonquin and Nipissing chiefs wrote to the Governor General describing their lands being taken on both sides of the Ottawa River and requesting settlement be limited to 40 arpents (7,673 feet) deep from the riverbanks. The letter included a belt of wampum and a map of their lands. **The Governor General did not respond** [^16].
- Early contact often began with Indigenous people helping settlers through their first winters, but relationships became exploitative through unfair trade, land theft, and illegal timber harvesting [^16].
- By the late 1800s, Algonquin people had been forced to abandon their traditional ways, with some relocating to reserves like Golden Lake (Pikw�kanag�n) [^16].

### Chief Pierre Shawinipinessi and the Bedford Reserve

One of the most significant stories directly tied to the Big Gull Lake region involves **Chief Pierre Shawinipinessi** (also known as Peter Stevens), an Algonquin leader and War of 1812 veteran [^16]:

- Born **1790** at Lac des Deux Montagnes (Kanesatake)
- By 1837, he had settled on an island in **Bob's Lake** (~30 km southeast of Big Gull Lake), purchasing supplies from local magistrate Benjamin Tett [^16]
- In **1842**, he petitioned Governor General Sir Charles Bagot for a **2,000-acre reserve** in the townships of Bedford, Oso, and South Sherbrooke, explaining that colonial settlement had made traditional hunting "precarious" and that his people had turned to growing corn and potatoes [^16]
- **Samuel P. Jarvis**, Superintendent of Indian Affairs, supported the petition, noting the tract was "quite secluded and thereby rendered peculiarly adapted for an Indian settlement" [^16]
- In **March 1844**, an Order in Council formally approved the reserve, granting a "License of Occupation during pleasure" [^16]
- Immediately, **loggers trespassed** on the land, cutting timber and threatening the Algonquin families. The trespassers constructed cabins on the site. Despite years of petitions and the intervention of local magistrates, the government failed to provide protection [^16]
- Shawinipinessi petitioned on **December 25, 1845**, reporting that loggers had returned and "threatened the lives of the Bedford Algonquins if they should interfere with them" [^16]
- The Superintendent of Indian Affairs described the community as surrounded by "lumberers, the most depraved of men" [^16]
- By **1861**, the new Superintendent of Indian Affairs **explicitly denied the reserve's existence**, despite it appearing in the official Bagot Commission report of 1844 and census records taken at the tract until at least 1851 [^16]
- Community members dispersed � some to **Ardoch** (just south of Big Gull Lake), others to **Pikw�kanag�n** (Golden Lake, where a reserve was established in 1873), and others to **Kitigan Zibi** (established 1851) [^16]
- Shawinipinessi lived to age **101**, his last years at Pikw�kanag�n. He was listed on the census there in 1881 and 1882 [^16]

The descendants of this community are today members of the **Shabot Obaadjiwan First Nation** and the **Ardoch Algonquin First Nation** � both with direct connections to the Big Gull Lake area. They continue to fight for their land and rights as First Nations people [^16].

## VII. Early Settler History

### Clarendon Township

- Planned and named in **1792** after a place in Wiltshire, England [^31][^32].
- The government commissioned **Ensign James Prendergast**, a retired British Army officer, to survey and settle the township [^32].
- The first **15 settlers** were granted land in **1826**, mostly near the centre of the township [^32].
- Prendergast established **four schools** between 1827�1835 and built a water-driven **sawmill and grist mill** near the Ottawa River [^32].
- Settlement came in two waves: 1825�1827 (halted when free land grants ended), and the late 1830s�1840s when the timber industry took off. The population doubled between 1840 and 1850 [^32].
- The **Municipality of Clarendon** was established in **1841**, with Thomas Corrigan as one of the first mayors [^32].

### Logging Era (1840s�1880s)

- Timber cutting transformed the landscape. Mature pine forests were harvested for **shipbuilding** until steel plates replaced wooden timbers in the 1880s [^19].
- Unsuitable wood was piled and burned for potash production, creating acrid smoke that drove wildlife from vast areas [^16].
- This directly caused **starvation** among Algonquin families who depended on hunting and trapping [^16].
- Chief Kaondinoketch described the situation in 1840: *"Our hunting grounds that are vast and extensive and once abounded in the richest furs and swarmed with deer of every description are now ruined. We tell you the truth, we now starve half the year through and our children, who were accustomed to being comfortably clothed, are now naked"* [^16].

### Mica Mining (1890s�early 1900s)

- Clarendon Township was an active **amber mica (phlogopite)** mining region in the late 1800s and early 1900s [^33].
- The **Tett Mine** was a notable operation � by 1905, the main pit had reached ~80 feet deep [^33].
- Mining was extremely hazardous: hand drilling, blasting with black powder, no hard hats or electrical lights, constant risk of unexploded charges [^33].
- Mica veins were unpredictable, often narrowing to nothing without warning [^33].
- The broader Frontenac area also saw **iron ore mining** � the Glendower company mined 12,000 tons between 1873�1880 [^34].

### The Kingston & Pembroke Railway (1871�1986)

- The **K&P Railway** (nicknamed the "Kick and Push") was incorporated in **1871** to connect Kingston with Pembroke, driven by the lumber and mining industries [^35].
- Construction began in 1872, pushing through extremely challenging rocky Shield terrain. Key milestones: Iron Junction (29 miles) by 1875, Sharbot Lake (47 miles) by 1876, Renfrew (~180 km) by 1884 [^35][^36].
- The railway served communities across Frontenac County, connecting mining and logging settlements to Kingston [^35].
- Mining was a significant impetus for the railway's establishment [^34].
- The K&P was **leased to Canadian Pacific Railway for 999 years** in 1912, with CPR gaining control January 1, 1913 [^35].
- Gradual abandonment began in the 1950s; the last section closed in **1986** [^35].
- The corridor is now a **180 km recreational trail** (the K&P Trail) connecting Kingston to Renfrew [^35][^37].

### Big Gull Lake Settlement

- European settlement around Big Gull Lake began in the **1800s** [^1].
- **Viking Lodge**, a pre-Confederation cabin from the late 1800s, still stands on the lake today [^1].
- **Kirk Cove** resort has operated for over 80 years, and in the 1950s�60s hosted professional **water-skiing shows** featuring human pyramids, barefoot skiing, and jumps. Co-owners Guy and Bonnie Weese were professional competitors at Cypress Gardens [^1].
- Until **1983**, most cottagers at the east end had to reach their properties **by boat**. The first north shore road system was built that year under John Belcham's leadership, branching off Greer Road [^17].
- The **Big Gull Lake East End Cottage Association (BGLEECA)** was formed in **1985** in response to a devastating **gypsy moth infestation** that devoured hardwood leaves, especially oak. About 100 cottagers attended the founding meeting at Phypers Holiday Haven on Labour Day weekend [^17].
- First officers: Harold Krieger (President), Robert Jelking (Vice-President), Stan Bowman (Treasurer), Dennis Tice (Secretary) [^17].
- Spraying programs continued through the late 1990s to handle subsequent **forest tent caterpillar** and **hemlock looper** infestations [^17].
- The association established a 13-area representative system covering ~140 cottages, created a cottage numbering system (the forerunner of the 911 system), and formed a **volunteer fire department** by 1990 [^17].
- A community well was drilled approximately 225 feet deep and opened in **1992** [^17].
- Fish stocking on Big Gull Lake occurred from **1916 to 1961**, including walleye plantings reaching as high as 2,000,000 fish in 1946 [^3].
- Since the late 1980s, cottager volunteers, Junior Rangers, and MNR personnel have placed **hundreds of tons of rubble** on spawning sites to rebuild walleye habitat [^17].
